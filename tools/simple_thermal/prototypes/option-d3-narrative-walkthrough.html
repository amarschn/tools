Building Blocks

Convection Coefficient
hconv = 31.67 W/m²K
▶
▶ How well does air pull heat from the fin surfaces?
Each fin channel acts like a small rectangular duct. Air enters at one end, warms as it flows along the hot fin walls, and exits the other side. The convection coefficient h measures how efficiently this happens: higher h means less temperature difference needed to reject a given amount of heat.
Here the flow is laminar (Re = 1213) and the channels are short enough that the thermal boundary layer never fully develops. That's actually helpful — the "entry effect" boosts heat transfer above the fully-developed limit. The solver uses the Muzychka & Yovanovich (2004) correlation, which smoothly blends the two regimes.
▶ Show me the math
Gz = Re · Pr · Dh/L = 1213 × 0.705 × 0.00804/0.10 = 68.8
Nu = (7.54³ + (1.841 · Gz1/3)³)1/3 = 9.50
hconv = Nu · kair / Dh = 9.50 × 0.0268 / 0.00804 = 31.67 W/m²K
Gz (Graetz number) = how thermally undeveloped the flow is. High Gz = stronger entry effects, higher Nu.
Nu (Nusselt number) = dimensionless heat transfer. The 7.54 term is the fully-developed floor; the Gz term adds entry-region boost.
Dh = hydraulic diameter of the rectangular channel = 2sH/(s+H) = 8.04 mm.
▶ When would this be wrong?
  • Crossflow or angled approach: This correlation assumes air enters aligned with the channel axis. If air hits the fins from the side, the actual h could be significantly different.
  • Impingement cooling: Air blowing straight down onto the fin tips is a completely different flow regime — this model doesn't apply.
  • Very short channels (L/Dh < 5): Entry-region correlations become less reliable when the channel is shorter than a few hydraulic diameters.
  • Non-uniform fin spacing: The model assumes all channels are identical. Warped or inconsistent fin spacing breaks this assumption.
Fin & Surface Efficiency
ηf = 91.0%, ηo = 91.7%
▶
▶ Are the fins actually doing their job?
Yes — 91% efficient. A fin works by conducting heat from its root (attached to the base) out to its tip, where it's convected to the air. But the tip is always cooler than the root because heat has to travel through the metal. Fin efficiency measures how much of the fin's area is thermally "useful" compared to an idealized fin at uniform base temperature.
At 91%, these fins are well-sized. You could make them taller for more area, but efficiency would drop. Rule of thumb: below ~70% efficiency, you're adding metal for diminishing returns. These fins still have room.
▶ Show me the math
m = √(heff · P / (k · Ac)) = √(32.42 × 0.202 / (200 × 1.0e-4)) = 18.1 m-1
m · Lc = 18.1 × 0.0305 = 0.552
ηf = tanh(0.552) / 0.552 = 0.910

ηo = 1 − (Af/At)(1 − ηf) = 1 − (0.090/0.098)(0.090) = 0.917
m = fin parameter. Captures the balance between how fast heat convects away (h) vs. how fast it conducts along the fin (k·Ac). Bigger m = steeper temperature drop along the fin.
Lc = corrected fin length (height + half thickness for tip convection) = 30.5 mm.
ηo = overall surface efficiency. Blends fin efficiency with the exposed base (which is at 100% effectiveness).
▶ When would this be wrong?
  • Bonded or press-fit fins: There's contact resistance at the fin root that this model ignores. Only extruded (integral) fins have perfect root contact.
  • Very thin fins: Manufacturing tolerances may cause actual cross-section to differ from nominal.
  • Non-uniform h: The model assumes uniform convection over the entire fin surface, but h actually varies along the channel length.
Friction & Losses
f = 0.01644, Kc = 0.143, Ke = 0.035
▶
▶ What resists the airflow?
Three things slow the air down:
1. Channel wall friction (dominant) — viscous drag along the full length of the fin channel walls. Depends on channel aspect ratio and flow regime.

2. Contraction at entry (Kc = 0.143) — air accelerates from the open approach area into narrower fin gaps, losing pressure in the process.

3. Expansion at exit (Ke = 0.035) — air decelerates back to the downstream region. Some kinetic energy is lost as turbulent mixing.
Together these produce 1.33 Pa of pressure drop — trivially low. The friction component dominates here because the channels are relatively long compared to their diameter (L/Dh = 12.4).
▶ Show me the math
β = s/H = 0.00464/0.030 = 0.155 (channel aspect ratio)
f·Re = 24(1 − 1.355β + 1.947β² − ...) = 19.93
f = 19.93/1213 = 0.01644

σ = Aopen/Afrontal = 0.813
Kc = 0.42(1−σ²) = 0.143   (Kays & London)
Ke = (1−σ)² = 0.035   (Borda-Carnot)
β = channel aspect ratio (spacing/height). Controls the f·Re product via Shah & London polynomial.
σ = open-area ratio. What fraction of the heatsink frontal face is open channel vs. solid fin.
Kc = contraction (entry) loss. From Kays & London (1984) for abrupt contraction into parallel plates.
Ke = expansion (exit) loss. Borda-Carnot model for sudden expansion.

    ▶ Will the heatsink choke the airflow?
    No. At 1.33 Pa, this heatsink creates negligible flow resistance. For context, typical small axial fans deliver 10–30 Pa, and even passive natural convection can drive a few Pa through a chimney effect. The flow is not limiting the thermal performance here.
    Because pressure drop is so low, you could add more fins (reducing spacing) without worrying about choking the flow. That would increase surface area and improve heat rejection — though with this design the bottleneck is the interface, not the sink.
