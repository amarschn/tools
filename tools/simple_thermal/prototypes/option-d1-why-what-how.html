Building Blocks

Air Properties
Tfilm = 33.6 °C (306.8 K)
▶
Why this matters
Air viscosity, density, and thermal conductivity all change with temperature. The solver evaluates them at the film temperature — the average of the base and ambient — because that best represents the conditions where heat transfer actually happens inside the fin channels.
Calculation
Tfilm = (Tb + T∞)/2 = (42.2 + 25.0)/2 = 33.6 °C
ρ = p/(Rair·T) = 101325/(287.1 × 306.8) = 1.151 kg/m³
μ via Sutherland law = 1.878×10-5 Pa·s
kair via Sutherland-type fit = 0.0268 W/m·K
Pr = μ·cp/k = 0.705
TbAverage base temperature (solved)42.2 °C
T∞Ambient / inlet air temperature (input)25.0 °C
ρAir density1.151 kg/m³
μDynamic viscosity1.878e-5 Pa·s
kairThermal conductivity of air0.0268 W/m·K
PrPrandtl number (ratio of momentum to thermal diffusivity)0.705
When this breaks down
  • Sutherland fits are valid 200–700 K. Current film temperature (307 K) is well within range.
  • At altitudes above ~3000 m, the assumed sea-level pressure (101.3 kPa) will overpredict density. Adjust ambient pressure if needed.
  • Film temperature averaging is a standard approximation. For very large ΔT (>80 °C), property variation across the boundary layer becomes significant.
Convection Coefficient
hconv = 31.67 W/m²K
▶
Why this correlation
The fin channels are treated as rectangular ducts with flow entering from one end. Because the flow is laminar (Re = 1213 < 2300) and the channels are relatively short, the thermal boundary layer hasn't fully developed by the exit. The Muzychka & Yovanovich (2004) correlation handles this developing-flow regime — it blends the fully-developed limit (Nu → 7.54 for long ducts) with a higher entry-region value that depends on the Graetz number. Short channels get more credit for entry effects, which is physically correct.
Calculation
Gz = Re · Pr · Dh/L = 1213 × 0.705 × 0.00804 / 0.10 = 68.8
Nu = (7.54³ + (1.841 · Gz1/3)³)1/3 = (429 + 5428)1/3 = 9.50
hconv = Nu · kair / Dh = 9.50 × 0.0268 / 0.00804 = 31.67 W/m²K
GzGraetz number — ratio of thermal entry length to channel length. High Gz = entry effects dominate.68.8
NuNusselt number — dimensionless heat transfer. Higher Nu = better convection.9.50
DhHydraulic diameter of fin channel = 2sH/(s+H)8.04 mm
LFin length in flow direction (= base length)100 mm
When this breaks down
  • This correlation assumes channel-aligned flow — air enters one end and exits the other. Crossflow, impingement, or angled approach invalidate it.
  • Assumes isothermal walls (constant fin temperature). Real fins have a temperature gradient — fin efficiency corrects for this separately.
  • At Re > 2300 the solver switches to the Gnielinski turbulent correlation. The transition zone (2300–3500) uses a blend.
  • Does not account for fin-tip flow spilling or inter-channel mixing in very short, widely-spaced fins.
Fin & Surface Efficiency
ηf = 91.0%, ηo = 91.7%
▶
Why this matters
A fin's tip is always cooler than its root because heat has to conduct along the fin before it can convect away. Fin efficiency (ηf) captures this penalty: 91% means the average fin temperature is 91% as effective as if the whole fin were at base temperature. Surface efficiency (ηo) blends this with the exposed base area (which is at full temperature) to give a single effectiveness for the entire heat-rejecting surface. When ηf drops below ~70%, adding more fin height is giving diminishing returns.
Calculation
heff = hconv + hrad = 31.67 + 0.746 = 32.42 W/m²K
m = √(heff · P / (k · Ac)) = √(32.42 × 0.202 / (200 × 0.0001)) = 18.1 m-1
m · Lc = 18.1 × 0.0305 = 0.552
ηf = tanh(0.552) / 0.552 = 0.500 / 0.552 = 0.910
ηo = 1 − (Af/At)(1−ηf) = 1 − (0.090/0.098)(1−0.910) = 0.917
mFin parameter — balances conduction along fin vs. convection from surface18.1 m-1
LcCorrected fin length (fin height + half thickness, for tip convection)30.5 mm
PFin perimeter (per unit depth into page)0.202 m
AcFin cross-section area (thickness × depth)1.0e-4 m²
kFin material conductivity (aluminum)200 W/m·K
When this breaks down
  • Uses the classic tanh fin model, which assumes 1D conduction along the fin and uniform h over all surfaces. Real h varies with position.
  • Assumes the base is isothermal. If spreading resistance is large, the base under the source is hotter than the edges, and local fin efficiency varies.
  • For bonded or press-fit fins, contact resistance at the fin root adds a penalty not captured here. Only integral (extruded) fins have perfect root contact.
  • Very thin fins (<0.5 mm) may have manufacturing tolerances that affect cross-section area.
Friction & Losses
f = 0.01644, Kc = 0.143, Ke = 0.035
▶
Why these three terms
Pressure drop through the fin array has three components: friction along the channel walls (the dominant term in long channels), contraction loss (Kc) as air accelerates from the open approach into narrower fin gaps, and expansion loss (Ke) as it decelerates at the exit. All three matter for predicting how much airflow a fan can push through — or equivalently, how much flow the heatsink "costs" the system.
Calculation
β = s/H = 0.00464/0.030 = 0.155 (channel aspect ratio)
f·Re = 24(1 − 1.355β + 1.947β² − ...) = 19.93
f = 19.93 / 1213 = 0.01644
σ = Aopen/Afrontal = 0.813
Kc = 0.42(1−σ²) = 0.42 × 0.339 = 0.143
Ke = (1−σ)² = 0.187² = 0.035
βChannel aspect ratio (spacing / height)0.155
σOpen-area ratio — fraction of frontal area that is open channel0.813
KcContraction (entry) loss coefficient0.143
KeExpansion (exit) loss coefficient (Borda-Carnot)0.035
When this breaks down
  • The f·Re product assumes fully developed laminar flow in a rectangular duct. For very short channels (L/Dh < 10), developing-flow friction is higher.
  • Kc uses a simplified constant (0.42) from Kays & London. Real contraction losses depend on exact inlet geometry (sharp-edge vs. rounded).
  • Ke uses the Borda-Carnot sudden-expansion model. Gradual diffusers would have lower loss.
  • These losses assume all channels receive equal flow. Non-uniform approach velocity or partial blockage will change the distribution.
