Thermal Path

  • ▶ Junction Temp Tj = 89.1 °C
    The predicted hottest point in the source/package is 89.1 °C, which is 10.9 °C below the 100 °C limit. The margin is modest — most of the temperature budget is consumed by the upstream path (interface + Rjc), not the heatsink.
    Tj = Tcase + Q · Rθ,jc = 74.1 + 50.0 × 0.30 = 89.1 °C
    Tcase
    Package mounting surface temperature — 74.1 °C (see below)
    Q
    Heat load — 50.0 W (input)
    Rθ,jc
    Junction-to-case resistance — 0.30 K/W (input, from package datasheet)
    Where the budget goes: Heatsink uses 17.2 °C (23%), spreading 6.9 °C (9%), interface 25.0 °C (33%), Rjc 15.0 °C (20%), leaving 10.9 °C margin (15%). The interface is the bottleneck.
    • ▶ Case Temp Tcase = 74.1 °C
      The outer mounting surface reaches 74.1 °C. The 25 °C rise from effective base to case is driven entirely by the thermal interface material (TIM) — this is the single largest temperature drop in the path.
      Tcase = Tbase,eff + Q · Rθ,cs = 49.1 + 50.0 × 0.50 = 74.1 °C
      Tbase,eff
      Effective base temp under the source (base + spreading) — 49.1 °C
      Rθ,cs
      Case-to-sink resistance from TIM — 0.50 K/W
      Reducing TIM bondline thickness or using higher-conductivity compound would have more impact than any heatsink geometry change.
      • ▶ Effective Base Tbase,eff = 49.1 °C
        Because the heat source (20 × 20 mm) is smaller than the base (100 × 70 mm), the metal directly under the source runs hotter than the base average. The spreading penalty adds 6.9 °C.
        Tbase,eff = Tb + Q · Rsp = 42.2 + 50.0 × 0.139 = 49.1 °C
        Rsp
        Spreading resistance — 0.139 K/W. Depends on source-to-base area ratio, base thickness, and conductivity. Uses the Yovanovich/Muzychka/Culham (1999) model.
        Applicability limits
        • Assumes a single centered rectangular source. Off-center or multiple sources need a different approach.
        • Assumes the base is thermally thick enough to spread. Very thin bases (< 2 mm) may not have room for lateral conduction.
        • ▶ Base Temp Tb = 42.2 °C
          The average base temperature is found by iteration: the solver adjusts Tb until total heat rejected by the fins and exposed base equals the 50 W load. At 42.2 °C the sink runs only 17.2 °C above ambient. The resulting Rsink = 0.344 K/W is well under the 0.700 K/W budget.
          Q = ηo · (hconv + hrad) · At · (Tb − T∞)
          50.0 = 0.917 × 32.42 × 0.098 × (Tb − 25.0)
          ⇒ ΔT = 17.2 °C ⇒ Tb = 42.2 °C
          Rθ,sink = ΔT/Q = 17.2/50 = 0.344 K/W
          ηo
          Overall surface efficiency — 91.7%. Accounts for fins being cooler at tips than roots. details
          hconv
          Convection coefficient — 31.67 W/m²K. From developing laminar flow correlation. details
          hrad
          Radiation coefficient — 0.746 W/m²K. Linearized Stefan-Boltzmann with fin view factor. details
          At
          Total exposed area (fins + base between fins) — 0.098 m²
          T∞
          Ambient air temperature — 25.0 °C (input)
          Applicability limits
          • Assumes an isothermal base. Valid for aluminum with moderate loads, but breaks down when a small source sits on a large thin base — hence the separate spreading correction above.
          • The solver iterates air properties at the film temperature (Tb+T∞)/2, so large ΔT (>80 °C) may reduce accuracy.
        • ▶ Spreading Rθ 0.139 K/W
          Spreading resistance accounts for the extra temperature rise because heat must fan out from a small source into a larger base. The smaller the source relative to the base, the larger this penalty.
          ε = √(As/Ap) = 0.224   (source-to-base area ratio)
          τ = t/√Ap = 0.056   (base thickness parameter)
          ψ = 0.990   (dimensionless spreading function)
          Rsp = ψ/(k·√(π·As)) = 0.990/(200 × 0.035) = 0.139 K/W
          ε
          Ratio of source to base characteristic lengths. Small ε = large spreading penalty.
          τ
          Normalized base thickness. Thicker base = more room for heat to spread laterally.
          ψ
          Dimensionless spreading parameter from the Yovanovich/Muzychka/Culham (1999) analytical model.
          k
          Base material conductivity — 200 W/m·K (aluminum)
      • ▶ Interface Rθ,cs 0.500 K/W
        The thermal interface material (TIM) between package and base contributes 0.500 K/W — consuming 25 °C at 50 W. This is the largest single resistance in the path.
        Rθ,cs = BLT / (kTIM · Acontact) = 0.0005 / (3.0 × 0.0004) = 0.417 K/W
        BLT
        Bondline thickness — 0.5 mm. Thinner = lower resistance, but harder to control.
        kTIM
        TIM thermal conductivity — 3.0 W/m·K. Typical thermal paste. Pads, greases, and solder range from 1 to 80+ W/m·K.
        Acontact
        Contact area — 400 mm² (20 × 20 mm source footprint).
        Three levers to reduce this: thinner bondline, higher-k TIM material, or larger contact area. Of these, switching from paste (3 W/m·K) to a gap pad (5–8 W/m·K) or indium solder (80 W/m·K) has the biggest single impact.
    • ▶Rθ,jc0.300 K/W (input)
  • ▶ Sink Resistance Rsink = 0.344 K/W  (budget: 0.700)
    The heatsink's thermal resistance is less than half the required budget. The sink is not the limiting factor — the upstream path (interface + Rjc) is.
    Rθ,req = (Tj,max−T∞)/Q − Rjc − Rcs = (100−25)/50 − 0.30 − 0.50 = 0.700 K/W
    Rθ,sink = (Tb−T∞)/Q = 17.2/50 = 0.344 K/W   ✓
  • ▶ Pressure Drop ΔP = 1.33 Pa
    Very low — even small fans deliver 10–30 Pa. The heatsink is not flow-limited. Three loss components: channel friction (dominant), contraction at entry, and expansion at exit.
    ΔP = (f·L/Dh + Kc + Ke) · ρ · Vch²/2
    = (0.01644 × 12.44 + 0.143 + 0.035) × 1.151 × 2.46²/2 = 1.33 Pa
    Vch
    Channel velocity — 2.46 m/s (approach flow divided by open area)
    L/Dh
    Channel length-to-diameter ratio — 12.4
  • ▶ Heat Split Convection 47.7 W (95%)  |  Radiation 2.3 W (5%)
    In forced flow, convection dominates overwhelmingly. Radiation contributes only 5% here. In natural convection the radiation share is typically 15–40%.
    Qconv = ηo · hconv · At · ΔT = 0.917 × 31.67 × 0.098 × 17.2 = 47.7 W
    Qrad = ηo · hrad · At · ΔT = 0.917 × 0.746 × 0.098 × 17.2 = 2.3 W

Reference Values

Shared intermediate quantities used by multiple results above. Click "derivation" to see how each was calculated.

Convection Coefficient
hconv = 31.67 W/m²K
derivation
Laminar developing flow in rectangular ducts (Re = 1213 < 2300). Uses Muzychka & Yovanovich (2004) — blends the fully-developed limit (Nu = 7.54) with an entry-region boost for short channels.

Gz = Re·Pr·Dh/L = 1213 × 0.705 × 0.00804/0.10 = 68.8
Nu = (7.54³ + (1.841·Gz1/3)³)1/3 = 9.50
h = Nu·kair/Dh = 9.50 × 0.0268/0.00804 = 31.67
Assumes channel-aligned flow, isothermal walls. Does not apply to crossflow, impingement, or angled approach. At Re > 2300 the solver switches to Gnielinski.
Radiation Coefficient
hrad = 0.746 W/m²K
derivation
Linearized Stefan-Boltzmann with an effective view factor for fin-to-fin radiation shielding.

Feff = s/(s+H) = 0.00464/0.0346 = 0.134
hrad = ε·σ·(Ts²+T∞²)(Ts+T∞)·Feff = 0.746
Feff = simplified view factor. Closely spaced tall fins see each other more than the environment, reducing net radiation.
Fin & Surface Efficiency
ηf = 91.0%, ηo = 91.7%
derivation
A fin's tip is cooler than its root. Efficiency captures this penalty. At 91%, these fins are well-sized — below ~70% means diminishing returns from taller fins.

m = √(heff·P/(k·Ac)) = 18.1 m-1
m·Lc = 18.1 × 0.0305 = 0.552
ηf = tanh(0.552)/0.552 = 0.910
ηo = 1−(Af/At)(1−ηf) = 0.917
Assumes uniform h over fin surface and perfect root contact (integral/extruded fins). Bonded or press-fit fins have additional root resistance.
Friction & Losses
f = 0.01644, Kc = 0.143, Ke = 0.035
derivation
Three components: wall friction (Shah & London rectangular duct), contraction at entry (Kays & London), expansion at exit (Borda-Carnot).

β = s/H = 0.155,   f·Re = 19.93,   f = 0.01644
σ = 0.813,   Kc = 0.42(1−σ²) = 0.143
Ke = (1−σ)² = 0.035
