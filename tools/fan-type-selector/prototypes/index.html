Result Layout Concepts

These change how the same selection result is read, but keep the chart ideas secondary.

Prototype A: Result Subtabs

Keeps the top recommendation shallow, then pushes the rest of the result into single-column sub-tabs inside the Results area: Overview, Hardware, Alternatives, and Technical Basis. This is the lowest-risk production refactor.

  • Best when the user still wants conventional in-page navigation.
  • Reduces scroll by only showing one result slice at a time.
  • Still feels like a normal engineering tool, not a custom app shell.
Open Prototype A

Prototype B: Drawer Workbench

Treats the result as a shallow decision dashboard. The main page stays short and calm; deeper packaging, alternatives, and technical basis open in a right-hand drawer.

  • Most aggressive at killing scroll.
  • Strongest for “check one thing, then close it” workflows.
  • More custom interaction, so higher implementation cost.
Open Prototype B

Prototype C: Compact Accordion

Keeps everything in one column, but turns the results into a tight accordion with a sticky section rail. This keeps the mental model simple while reducing visible height.

  • Best if the team wants a conservative UI move.
  • Feels close to the current tool, but much calmer.
  • Still allows a full-page reading flow when needed.
Open Prototype C

Prototype D: Verdict Then Evidence

The decision is the opening sentence. A numbered funnel shows how four types narrowed to one, with each step expanding to show the reasoning. Numbers appear only where they support a specific step, not in a detached grid.

  • Makes the "why" impossible to miss — it's the first thing on the page.
  • Linear funnel replaces nested tabs entirely.
  • Each step is self-contained: question, answer, optional evidence.
Open Prototype D

Prototype E: Score Waterfall

All four types visible at once with stacked horizontal bars showing what each scored and why. Click any row to expand its hardware details and trade-offs. The visual gap between bars IS the explanation.

  • Score components are immediately visual — no table hunting.
  • Comparison is always on screen; details expand inline.
  • Works well for users who think in relative terms ("how close is #2?").
Open Prototype E

Prototype F: Guided Narrative

Reads top-to-bottom like a decision memo with a timeline spine. Each step shows its conclusion on the fold line; expand to see the supporting evidence. No tabs, no metric grid — just the story of how the decision was made.

  • Most natural reading order — mirrors how an engineer would explain it verbally.
  • Ranked comparison cards with hardware numbers inline, not in a separate view.
  • Callout boxes flag the one or two things that could overturn the recommendation.
Open Prototype F

Family Map Concepts

These are direct answers to the current Family Map weakness. The emphasis is on showing where the bounds come from and separating territory definition from temporary zoom level.

Prototype G: Explicit Bounds Bands

Replaces the irregular family polygons with declared comfort and fade bands. The user can read the flow and pressure ranges directly instead of inferring them from a freehand outline.

  • Strongest answer to “what defines the bounds?”
  • Turns the map into an inspectable data model, not hidden vertices.
  • Most production-friendly if we want the chart to look cleaner fast.
Open Prototype G

Prototype H: Focus Window + Inset

Keeps a global family field, but adds a visible viewport mode. The inset shows the full screening space while the main chart can zoom around the current duty point.

  • Strongest answer to “why are the axis limits here?”
  • Separates permanent territory bounds from temporary viewing focus.
  • Best if we want to preserve the map feel without arbitrary-looking extents.
Open Prototype H

Cordier Diagram Concepts

These prototypes target the Cordier chart specifically. The goal is to get closer to the classic Balje-style visual language seen in legacy turbomachinery references: swapped axes, a descending optimum locus, efficiency islands, and simpler architecture markers.

Prototype I: Classic Islands

Sepia paper treatment with classic contour lines and the more conventional Cordier orientation: specific speed on x, specific diameter on y.

  • Closest to the linked Concepts NREC reference.
  • Feels like a legacy turbomachinery chart immediately.
  • Least “app-like” of the three directions.
Open Prototype I

Prototype J: Type Bars

Monochrome technical plate that separates the efficiency structure from explicit specific-speed bars for FC, BC, mixed-flow, and axial architectures.

  • Strongest if the chart should behave like a selection diagram first.
  • Makes the family ranges easier to inspect directly.
  • Very clean, but less atmospheric than the classic contour look.
Open Prototype J

Prototype K: Filled Atlas

A middle ground between legacy textbook styling and a production-ready digital chart, using restrained filled efficiency islands and direct family anchors.

  • Most likely to be adaptable into the live tool.
  • Still recognizably Cordier, but more polished on-screen.
  • Balances readability and visual character.
Open Prototype K

Balje Diagram Concepts

These prototypes answer the next question directly: if Cordier and Balje can share the same N_s-D_s axes, what makes them feel different? The short answer is that Balje usually shows a broader efficiency field and performance atlas around the optimum line.

Prototype L: Balje Line Contours

A classical contour-sheet take: iso-efficiency lines across the whole field with the optimum Cordier path drawn as one heavy line inside it.

  • Most literal Balje-style prototype.
  • Best for showing that Cordier is one path inside a larger map.
  • Feels the most textbook-like.
Open Prototype L

Prototype M: Balje Filled Field

Filled efficiency islands plus a second chart showing peak efficiency along the optimum line as a function of specific speed.

  • Most informative of the three.
  • Helps separate field behavior from the optimum-locus slice.
  • Probably the best bridge to production UI.
Open Prototype M

Prototype N: Cordier vs Balje, Same Axes

Side-by-side comparison that uses identical axes in both charts so the difference in chart meaning is obvious rather than abstract.

  • Built specifically to answer the current confusion.
  • Left panel is the Cordier-style reading; right is the Balje-style field.
  • Best teaching prototype of the set.
Open Prototype N

Reference-Driven Rebuilds

These are the stronger second-pass attempts driven by the actual published references rather than by my earlier generic interpretation. They aim to reproduce the visual logic of the source figures more faithfully.

Prototype O: Published Turbine Atlas

A Concepts-NREC-inspired turbine sheet with dense black contour families, region labels, and the older atlas feel of the published figure.

  • Most faithful to the shared turbine reference image.
  • Intentionally looks like a book figure, not an app chart.
  • Best benchmark for “does this feel like the literature?”
Open Prototype O

Prototype P: MDPI Low-Re Overlay

Reframes the chart around the MDPI paper’s actual idea: a standard Balje map with modified low-Re Cordier lines layered on top.

  • Most faithful to the MDPI Figure 8 logic.
  • Shows why “Balje map” and “Cordier line” are not the same thing.
  • Useful if low-Re scaling matters to the design discussion.
Open Prototype P

Prototype S: Balje Diagram (Physics-Correct)

The production candidate. Efficiency comes from a single formula — ηpeak(Ns) humps along the Cordier line, decayed off-ridge by a Gaussian — so family peaks sit on the line by construction and the "islands" emerge rather than being drawn. Includes a bottom strip showing the ηpeak profile along the Cordier line.

  • Uses a reusable balje-physics.js module (single source of truth for parameters).
  • All family anchors sit on the Cordier line by construction — no drift.
  • Easy to drop into the main tool's Balje tab.
Open Prototype S

Prototype R: Balje Atlas (Hero)

A final hero attempt. Each machine family is an independent bivariate-Gaussian efficiency island on the Ns–Ds plane. The Cordier optimum is a monotone spline through the four family peaks.

  • Looks like the published Balje figure, not a ridge with banding.
  • Uses a soft cream-to-steel-blue fill with labelled line contours.
  • Includes duty-point crosshair and per-family anchor markers.
Open Prototype R

Prototype Q: Published-Style Family Chart

A production-minded compromise that applies the published chart language to the fan selector’s family-screening use case.

  • Most likely handoff into the real tool.
  • Uses traditional contours without losing the family-screening layer.
  • Better candidate than the first-pass Balje mockups.
Open Prototype Q
